The Grand Canyon

History of the Canyon

The Grand Canyon is located in northwestern Arizona and covers over 1.2 million acres. Due to the evidence the canyon holds, it is greatly studied by geologists who have interest in the Paleozoic, Mesozoic and Cenozoic eras. The Colorado River began creating the canyon hundreds of million years ago and the canyon encompasses 277 miles of it. Currently the canyon is 6,000 feet deep at its deepest point and 18 miles wide at its widest point.

Over 800 years ago, the ancient civilization of the Puebloan people inhabited the grand canyon, leaving behind dwellings, food storage areas, gardens and other artifacts. To this day there are still some tribes that live in the canyon among the wild.

In 1857, the Grand Canyon was nicknamed “The Great Unknown,” because it was the last unexplored area of the United States. Explorers Joseph Christmas Ives and John Wesley Powell were two of the first documented people to enter the canyon.

Visiting the Canyon

There are two main areas that visitors go to to visit the canyon: the North Rim and the South Rim. The map below shows some of the popular lookout points on both the North and the South Rim.

The South Rim is more popular, so can be hard to book accommodations during peak season, however this area is open year round. In addition to access to many great viewpoints of the canyon, the South Rim offers free shuttle busses that run on natural gas for easy access many sightseeing locations and museums.

The North Rim is visited less and offers a quieter experience. Though it attracts less tourists, it still offers plenty of options for camping, lodging and hiking in the canyon. This area of the park is open mid-May to mid-October.
